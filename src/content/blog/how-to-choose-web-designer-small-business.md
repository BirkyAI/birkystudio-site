You've decided your business needs a website — great call. But now comes the tricky part: finding someone to actually build it.

Google "web designer near me" and you'll get thousands of results. Freelancers charging $200. Agencies quoting $20,000. Website builders promising you can do it yourself in an afternoon. So how do you cut through the noise and find the right fit?

Here's a practical guide based on what we've seen work (and fail) for small businesses.

## Know What You Actually Need

Before you start shopping around, get clear on your goals. Not every business needs the same thing.

**Ask yourself these questions:**

- Do I just need an online presence (hours, location, services)?
- Do I want to generate leads or bookings online?
- Am I selling products and need an online store?
- Do I need to show up in Google searches?
- Do I need the site in multiple languages?

The answers will narrow your options fast. A restaurant that just needs a menu and directions has very different needs than a real estate agency that needs property listings and lead capture.

## What to Look For in a Web Designer

### 1. They Ask About Your Business Goals First

A good designer doesn't start by showing you templates. They start by asking questions: What does your business do? Who are your customers? What do you want visitors to do on your site?

If someone jumps straight to "pick a color scheme" without understanding your business, that's a red flag.

### 2. They Build for Mobile First

Over 60% of web traffic now comes from phones. Your site needs to look and work perfectly on a small screen. Ask to see their portfolio on your phone, not just on a laptop.

### 3. They Understand SEO

A beautiful website that nobody can find is worthless. Your designer should know the basics: fast loading speed, proper headings, meta descriptions, and how to structure pages so Google understands them. You can learn more about this in our [5 Simple SEO Tips](/blog/5-simple-seo-tips-small-business) post.

### 4. They Offer Ongoing Support

Websites aren't "set it and forget it." They need updates, security patches, and occasional changes. Ask what happens after launch — do they offer maintenance? How much does it cost?

### 5. They Have Real Client Examples

Look for actual business websites in their portfolio, not just demo sites. Better yet, ask for client references. A designer proud of their work will happily connect you with past clients.

## What to Avoid

**🚩 The "everything is included" package for $300.** You get what you pay for. Cheap sites often use outdated templates, have no SEO, and load slowly.

**🚩 No contract or scope document.** Always get the deliverables, timeline, and costs in writing before you pay anything.

**🚩 They can't explain things simply.** If a designer can't explain what they're doing in plain language, they may not understand it themselves — or they're hiding complexity that will cost you later.

**🚩 They lock you in.** Your website should be yours. Make sure you own your domain, hosting, and content. If a designer insists on hosting everything under their account, walk away.

**🚩 No mention of site speed or performance.** Google ranks fast sites higher. A designer who doesn't care about loading speed doesn't care about your visibility.

## Freelancer vs. Agency: Which Is Right for You?

| | Freelancer | Agency |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost** | Lower ($500–$3,000) | Higher ($3,000–$15,000+) |
| **Speed** | Often faster | More process, longer timelines |
| **Communication** | Direct with the builder | Account managers, more structure |
| **Best for** | Simple sites, tight budgets | Complex projects, ongoing needs |

For most small businesses, a skilled freelancer or a small studio (like us!) hits the sweet spot — personal attention without enterprise pricing.

## The Right Questions to Ask Before Hiring

Here's a quick checklist for your first conversation:

1. Can I see 3 live websites you've built for businesses like mine?
2. How will the site perform on mobile devices?
3. What's included in the price? What costs extra?
4. How long will the project take from start to finish?
5. Will I be able to make simple edits myself?
6. Do you handle hosting and domain setup?
7. What happens if something breaks after launch?
8. Do you build with SEO in mind?

## DIY vs. Professional: When It Makes Sense to Hire

Let's be honest — tools like Wix and Squarespace have gotten pretty good. If you're a brand-new business with zero budget, a DIY site is better than no site at all.

**But consider hiring a professional when:**

- You need to look credible to win bigger clients
- You want to show up in Google searches
- You need features like booking, e-commerce, or lead forms
- You don't have time to learn a website builder
- You want a site that grows with your business

A professional site pays for itself when it brings in even one or two extra customers per month.
